Major League Baseball

In the summer of 2024, I was a test engineer intern at Major League Baseball. I joined baseball enterprise, a large team of software engineers and product managers responsible for league-wide internal applications. Within it are various sub-teams, which I bounced between depending on the applications I was working on. Some projects I worked on include:

  • EBIS (Electronic Baseball Information System), which manages every major league organization's players and staff, including their minor league affiliates
  • MLB's umpire scheduling app
  • MLB's replay operations center app

... and various other applications since I returned to MLB in August 2025. As a software test engineer at MLB headquarters in New York City (directly across the street from Radio City Music Hall), I am responsible for creating (coding) test automation for enterprise applications; specifically API, UI, database, and load testing. Recently I have been contributing to MiLB Hub, the primary application for minor league operations which spans 120 clubs, as well as teams outside of enterprise, including Commerce (MLB TV subscriptions) and Fan Identity (Fan logins for public applications).

One of the most recent events at the office was the Lunar New Year Celebration, hosted by MLB Asian, one of the many employee resource groups (ERGs).

Feel free to reach out if you have any questions about MLB or the sports industry in general.

Software Engineering

I knew I wanted to go into computer science since high school. I took Computer Science I & II, and AP Computer Science. However, athough I was strong in math, I also had a passion for music. As someone with a creative heart, I integrated my studies with music and music technology.

Before MLB, I did a co-op as a QA (quality assurance) automation intern at Entrust Corporation (a cybersecurity company), where I used tools to create UI automation to test their internal commerce web portal. As a co-op, I worked alongside a small QA team for 6 months in Concord, MA.

After that, I continued as a Northeastern student and worked on various projects. There, I focused on game development and digital audio signal processing. I did two capstone projects, one on game audio and the other on game AI. Feel free to visit my main page to see all my projects.

For those interested in pursuing computer science, I one core piece of advice for high school students that could give you become successful in today's world. Always ensure you have a direction you want to go in. Software is a huge field, and there are many specialties, whether that's IT, data engineering, AI, test engineering (like me), etc. Focus on a particular industry you are interested in; for me, that was entertainment. Develop your skills and become strong in one specific area. On the other hand, you should also be open to discovery and be able to pivot. An important part of your journey is learning about yourself, so be prepared to explore.

Feel free to reach out if you have any questions about my journey as a software engineer.

Music Technology

Music has become a part of who I am. I played the flute all through high school and freshman year of college. During my junior and senior years at Herricks, I also focused on music composition, which I would later continue to do and integrate with songwriting and technology.

Music has been changed by technology in many ways, and at Northeastern I wanted to focus on its intersection with music. My major focuses were game audio, digital signal processing, music and mathematics, and acoustics. I was Vice President of NUSound, the Northeastern Chapter of the Acoustical Society of America.

As part of my music technology capstone, I integrated sound into a video game called Heqet using audio middleware, composed the game's soundtrack, and researched underwater acoustics. If you're curious, you can read the paper here.

In summer 2025, I was a conference assistant at the 50th anniversary of the International Computer Music Conference (ICMC), hosted by Northeastern University in collaboration with New England Conservatory, Berklee College of Music, Massachusetts Institute of Technology, and Emerson College. The conference was made up of many performances and research papers from all around the world, from students to veteran professionals. I personally was responsible for preparing the programs and helping to setup the concerts. Above is a photo of one of the performances. You can check out the conference website here.

To this day, I write songs under the name Cerii, looking to release my album Summer Night Rain this year.

Northeastern University

Northeastern was my first choice college for a variety of reasons.

(1) Northeastern has its co-op program. If you are not familiar with what a co-op, it's essentially an internship where, instead of being a part of a full internship program, you work full-time hours for an entire semester. You do not take any classes, and most of these co-ops are paid. I was a co-op at a cybersecurity company, Entrust Corporation, whose office was in Concord, MA. I commuted there every work day for 6 months from my dorm on campus. It is one of the best opportunities for students to get work experience — that is, both practical skills and familiarity with work culture.

(2) Northeastern has 200+ combined majors. This is different from a double major, which you may be more familiar with. A combined major is made up of two disciplines, but its curriculum is integrative. That means that you can take a class that entails both of your two chosen disciplines. I was a computer science and music combined major with a music technology concentration, and Northeastern has a whole catalog of courses specific to music technology. If you want to hone in on a specific interdisciplinary area of expertise in your career, this is a fantastic way of doing so. Some examples of combined majors include:

  • Computer Science and Behavioral Neuroscience
  • Cybersecurity and Criminal Justice
  • Computer Science and Design
  • Economics and Business Administration

Northeastern is the first university to have a dedicated College of Computer Sciences (which includes Data Science, Information Science, Cybersecurity, etc.), which is separate from the College of Engineering (Computer Engineering, Mechanical Engineering, Chemical Engineering, etc.) and the College of Science (Biology, Mathematics, Physics, etc.)

(3) Northeastern is located in Boston. There are plenty of universities in Boston, but Northeastern's Boston campus gives both a campus-like feel while still very much in the middle of the city. Boston is a city of opportunity, art, and community. I would say Northeastern's campus is iconic and astoundingly beautiful.

Here is my core piece of advice for high school seniors, regardless of which school you choose to go to. Always look to meet people and cherish those relationships. College flies by fast, and there you can meet your closest friends or partner, as well as network with professionals in various industries. Always stay true to yourself and learn to enjoy life. Every connection is valuable.

Feel free to reach out if you have any questions about Northeastern or college in general.
